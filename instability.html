Instability

Many Californians struggle to make ends meet

Food assistance is a way of coping with a challenge that almost everyone is familiar with: making ends meet. Despite its size and economic diversity, California is the fourth most expensive place to live in the country, after Hawaii, Washington DC, and New York. While eligibility for SNAP is based on the federal poverty line (which is the same across all states) Californians has the fourth highest cost of living in the nation.[1] If cost of living is taken into account, California has the highest poverty rate of any state.[2]

These economic circumstances mean that many people have no buffer if they suddenly incur a large expense or lose income. It is not surprising that the most common dilemma GetCalFresh applicants mention in their applications is not being able to afford food after paying for rent, utilities, and gas or public transit fare to get to work. In their applications for food assistance, about three in five of our applicants report having less than $100 on hand after monthly expenses.

Working parents use CalFresh to feed their families

Most of the people receiving CalFresh benefits who can work, do work. But they often work in low-wage jobs like retail, the restaurant industry, home health, or construction. These jobs often have unsteady hours that can leave workers in financially unstable situations. They also lack benefits and flexibility, which can mean frequent job losses and gaps between jobs if transportation or child care falls through. When food assistance is taken away from parents, that means fewer groceries, which negatively impacts the health of the whole family.

Despite their efforts, parents often struggle to provide healthy food for their kids. The median GetCalFresh parent works 38 hours a week, earning $14.70 an hour. In fact, 66% of GetCalFresh households with children include someone who is working. Nationally, 65% of SNAP households with children include someone who is working.[1]

The testimonials of these parents echo the research findings. In interviews with 90 families with children receiving SNAP benefits, academics found that about half restricted their own eating to make sure their children would have enough food.footnote In the U.S. in 2017, 15.7% of households with children were food insecure.[1][2] In about 45% of these households (or 7% of all U.S. households), adults reported skipping meals or cutting the size of a meal in the last 30 days.[3]

Young people rely on CalFresh when trying to finish school or start their careers

For college and graduate students, paying for food and rent can make it that much harder to finish school. Recent college or high school grads who are just starting their careers often aren't paid enough to meet their basic needs.

{% include quotes.html pageId="instability" contentId="students" %}

Students often take desperate measures to stay fed: they join clubs for the food, participate in research for cash, and ask friends to swipe them into the dining hall. In some counties, a large share of the applicants GetCalFresh sees are students. For example, students account for 60.7% of applications in Santa Cruz County and 58% of applications in Yolo County.

For recent high school or college grads entering the workforce, CalFresh serves as a bridge that gets them through the least secure, lowest-paid time of their careers. This is easy to see in the data: GetCalFresh applicants below age 30 work four fewer hours a week, and earn two dollars less per hour, than applicants in their thirties (the latter work a median of 36 hours a week at $15 per hour). Moreover, 46.1% of applicants under age 30 live by themselves, compared to only 28.5% of applicants in their thirties.[1]

Many seniors need help buying food, but are especially affected by stigma

Saving enough to have a stable retirement income is more and more challenging these days. Seniors, who are often on this kind of fixed income, can especially benefit from food assistance. Yet they represent the population with the greatest gap between their eligibility for CalFresh and their enrollment in the program, due in large part to stigma. [1][2][3][4] Perhaps unsurprisingly, many GetCalFresh applicants who are seniors describe a recent job loss or seeking employment as a reason for applying.

{% include quotes.html pageId="instability" contentId="seniors" %}

Of applicants who are 60 or older and working, two in three work at least 20 hours a week. In California, the average monthly benefit for a senior is $158, making it especially worth their while to apply.[1] Yet the gap between senior participation and eligibility suggests that seniors are one of the most reluctant groups to seek out food assistance. Nationally, SNAP reaches 85% of all eligible individuals, but only 45% of eligible seniors footnote. In California, the disparity is worse: SNAP reaches 70% of eligible people, but only 18% of eligible seniors.[2]
