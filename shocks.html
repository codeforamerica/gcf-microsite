Common Shocks

Common shocks that create a need for CalFresh

Up to this point, we've focused on stories from some of our largest groups of applicants: working families, students or young people starting their careers, and seniors. They convey stress. Their households have no slack in their incomes â€” they already cannot keep up with the bare necessities. A single car accident, medical emergency, or unexpected layoff can break the budget for months, often forcing them to choose between food, housing, or medical care. In this section, we examine some of the common shocks that lead already vulnerable individuals to seek out CalFresh.

Academic researchers lump shocks that lead to food (and financial) insecurity into simple categories: as sharp increases in expenses or reductions in income.[1][2] In this section, we'll describe a few of the more common shocks our applicants describe. They include losing a job, the high costs of illness or injury, or having to stay home to take care of a family member. Natural disasters, while less frequent, can also be a shock - in recent months, thousands of people have applied due to being displaced by California's wildfires.

Job loss is the most common reason for applying

For people who are already struggling financially, a job loss or reduction in hours is often the shock that makes food assistance necessary.

{% include quotes.html pageId="shocks" contentId="job_loss" %}

People who apply for food assistance often have experienced a hardship that causes them to slip into poverty. Roughly 2 in 5 households that fall into poverty do so because of the loss of a job. Nearly half (47%) of households that exit poverty will do so because of a new job. [1]

When transitioning between jobs, CalFresh reduces stress around food availability and the pressure to make costly trade-offs to get by. Clients share stories about dire choices they have to make like selling a car in a rush or using rent money for food and being evicted. The most common reason applicants tell us for no longer needing food assistance is that they went from unemployed to employed, found a better job, or got more hours. CalFresh provides the stability to find more regular hours, or a higher paying job.

Illness and injury are often destabilizing events

Not everyone has a plan when serious illness or disability strikes. Taking time away from work and managing medical bills add to the financial strain of Californians living close to poverty line.

{% include quotes.html pageId="shocks" contentId="medical" %}

Twenty-eight percent of non-elderly adults on SNAP have a disability.[1] Yet, because most disability benefit applications (SSI or SSDI) are rejected on their first attempt and it often takes two years to get approved, many rely on SNAP in the interim. Research shows that of people who are disabled and on SNAP, two in five do not receive SSI or SSDI.[2]

Choosing between caring for a family member and working

GetCalFresh applicants often describe how much time they spend caring for a disabled spouse, child, or older relative. Caretaking can be a full-time job, but is often unpaid and lacks benefits. It leaves many with an inflexible schedule to find times they can earn income.

{% include quotes.html pageId="shocks" contentId="caretaking" %}

It's hard to find care for disabled children and middle-aged adults. Long-term care facilities are expensive, and limit options for older family members. Family members providing caretaking support often have to cut back on their work hours and income. This decreases a family's ability to buy healthy food.[1] As a result, households with disabled people are two to three times more likely to be food insecure.[2]

Natural disasters often hit the poor the hardest

California is prone to natural disasters like fires, droughts, and earthquakes. The impact of disaster on low-income communities can be catastrophic and reveal how many people were financially insecure. They may have had enough money to get by, but limited insurance or a very small rainy day fund. Many are forced to use already limited assets to buffer losses like moving to a new area to find a job or home to rent. Following a disaster, aid normally comes in the form of FEMA, unemployment insurance, and food assistance. People who were better off financially often recover more quickly, while others need more support to get back on their feet.
