Who actually receives food assistance?

A fresh look at the facts

Californians who receive food assistance come from all backgrounds, but many share a similar story: they were already struggling with financial instability when they experienced a common shock. CalFresh helps people through challenging times in their lives. This site shares their stories.

At Code for America, we run GetCalFresh.org, which helps more than 20,000 households apply for food assistance every month. We know that it's very difficult to overcome stigma and seek out help, even for people who are experiencing hunger. Despite their hardships, many of our users still feel compelled to describe the reluctance, and often shame, they feel about asking for help. They might mention they waited too long to apply, they have never asked for help before, or they hope to be off of food assistance as quickly as possible.

The stigma our users experience arises from myths about food assistance, which have a deep resonance in American culture. It only takes an occasional sensational anecdote for these myths to persist. Through this site, we will counteract those myths with real stories from GetCalFresh applicants, in their own words, that ground us in the facts about food assistance. We'll showcase real, representative stories of people seeking and receiving assistance, as well as relevant findings from rigorous research. We know that, as one of our users put it, "the less fortunate are invisible to so many people." Our aim is to surface their stories, with dignity and respect, and to make them visible.

{% include stats.html sectionId="intro" %}

Most GetCalFresh users were barely making ends meet when they were tipped into crisis by an unexpected expense or job loss.[1] The first set of stories on this site exemplify the precarious financial lives of many Californians. Once cost of living is taken into account, California is the state with the highest poverty rate.[2]

The second set of stories describes common shocks or adverse events that lead people to apply for food assistance. An illness, injury, loss of a job, a rent increase, or even a car breakdown might be enough to force a financially vulnerable household to begin making choices between food and other necessities.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="intro" contentId="self_sufficiency" %}
{% include hero_header.html sectionId="instability" title="Instability" subtitle="Many Californians struggle to make ends meet" %}

Food assistance is a way of coping with a challenge that almost everyone is familiar with: making ends meet. Despite its size and economic diversity, California is the fourth most expensive place to live in the country, after Hawaii, Washington DC, and New York. While eligibility for SNAP is based on the federal poverty line (which is the same across all states) Californians has the fourth highest cost of living in the nation.[1] If cost of living is taken into account, California has the highest poverty rate of any state.[2]

{% include stats.html sectionId="instability" %}

These economic circumstances mean that many people have no buffer if they suddenly incur a large expense or lose income. It is not surprising that the most common dilemma GetCalFresh applicants mention in their applications is not being able to afford food after paying for rent, utilities, and gas or public transit fare to get to work. In their applications for food assistance, about three in five of our applicants report having less than $100 on hand after monthly expenses.

Working parents use CalFresh to feed their families

Most of the people receiving CalFresh benefits who can work, do work. But they often work in low-wage jobs like retail, the restaurant industry, home health, or construction. These jobs often have unsteady hours that can leave workers in financially unstable situations. They also lack benefits and flexibility, which can mean frequent job losses and gaps between jobs if transportation or child care falls through. When food assistance is taken away from parents, that means fewer groceries, which negatively impacts the health of the whole family.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="instability" contentId="parents" %}

Despite their efforts, parents often struggle to provide healthy food for their kids. The median GetCalFresh parent works 38 hours a week, earning $14.70 an hour. In fact, 66% of GetCalFresh households with children include someone who is working. Nationally, 65% of SNAP households with children include someone who is working.[1]

The testimonials of these parents echo the research findings. In interviews with 90 families with children receiving SNAP benefits, academics found that about half restricted their own eating to make sure their children would have enough food.footnote In the U.S. in 2017, 15.7% of households with children were food insecure.[1][2] In about 45% of these households (or 7% of all U.S. households), adults reported skipping meals or cutting the size of a meal in the last 30 days.[3]

Young people rely on CalFresh when trying to finish school or start their careers

For college and graduate students, paying for food and rent can make it that much harder to finish school. Recent college or high school grads who are just starting their careers often aren't paid enough to meet their basic needs.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="instability" contentId="students" %}

Students often take desperate measures to stay fed: they join clubs for the food, participate in research for cash, and ask friends to swipe them into the dining hall. In some counties, a large share of the applicants GetCalFresh sees are students. For example, students account for 60.7% of applications in Santa Cruz County and 58% of applications in Yolo County.

For recent high school or college grads entering the workforce, CalFresh serves as a bridge that gets them through the least secure, lowest-paid time of their careers. This is easy to see in the data: GetCalFresh applicants below age 30 work four fewer hours a week, and earn two dollars less per hour, than applicants in their thirties (the latter work a median of 36 hours a week at $15 per hour). Moreover, 46.1% of applicants under age 30 live by themselves, compared to only 28.5% of applicants in their thirties.[1]

Many seniors need help buying food, but are especially affected by stigma

Saving enough to have a stable retirement income is more and more challenging these days. Seniors, who are often on this kind of fixed income, can especially benefit from food assistance. Yet they represent the population with the greatest gap between their eligibility for CalFresh and their enrollment in the program, due in large part to stigma. [1][2][3][4] Perhaps unsurprisingly, many GetCalFresh applicants who are seniors describe a recent job loss or seeking employment as a reason for applying.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="instability" contentId="seniors" %}

Of applicants who are 60 or older and working, two in three work at least 20 hours a week. In California, the average monthly benefit for a senior is $158, making it especially worth their while to apply.[1] Yet the gap between senior participation and eligibility suggests that seniors are one of the most reluctant groups to seek out food assistance. Nationally, SNAP reaches 85% of all eligible individuals, but only 45% of eligible seniors footnote. In California, the disparity is worse: SNAP reaches 70% of eligible people, but only 18% of eligible seniors.[2]

{% include hero_header.html sectionId="shocks" title="Common Shocks" subtitle="Common shocks that create a need for CalFresh" %}

Up to this point, we've focused on stories from some of our largest groups of applicants: working families, students or young people starting their careers, and seniors. They convey stress. Their households have no slack in their incomes â€” they already cannot keep up with the bare necessities. A single car accident, medical emergency, or unexpected layoff can break the budget for months, often forcing them to choose between food, housing, or medical care. In this section, we examine some of the common shocks that lead already vulnerable individuals to seek out CalFresh.

Academic researchers lump shocks that lead to food (and financial) insecurity into simple categories: as sharp increases in expenses or reductions in income.[1][2] In this section, we'll describe a few of the more common shocks our applicants describe. They include losing a job, the high costs of illness or injury, or having to stay home to take care of a family member. Natural disasters, while less frequent, can also be a shock - in recent months, thousands of people have applied due to being displaced by California's wildfires.

{% include stats.html sectionId="shocks" %}

Job loss is the most common reason for applying

For people who are already struggling financially, a job loss or reduction in hours is often the shock that makes food assistance necessary.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="shocks" contentId="job_loss" %}

People who apply for food assistance often have experienced a hardship that causes them to slip into poverty. Roughly 2 in 5 households that fall into poverty do so because of the loss of a job. Nearly half (47%) of households that exit poverty will do so because of a new job. [1]

When transitioning between jobs, CalFresh reduces stress around food availability and the pressure to make costly trade-offs to get by. Clients share stories about dire choices they have to make like selling a car in a rush or using rent money for food and being evicted. The most common reason applicants tell us for no longer needing food assistance is that they went from unemployed to employed, found a better job, or got more hours. CalFresh provides the stability to find more regular hours, or a higher paying job.

Illness and injury are often destabilizing events

Not everyone has a plan when serious illness or disability strikes. Taking time away from work and managing medical bills add to the financial strain of Californians living close to poverty line.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="shocks" contentId="medical" %}

Twenty-eight percent of non-elderly adults on SNAP have a disability.[1] Yet, because most disability benefit applications (SSI or SSDI) are rejected on their first attempt and it often takes two years to get approved, many rely on SNAP in the interim. Research shows that of people who are disabled and on SNAP, two in five do not receive SSI or SSDI.[2]

Choosing between caring for a family member and working

GetCalFresh applicants often describe how much time they spend caring for a disabled spouse, child, or older relative. Caretaking can be a full-time job, but is often unpaid and lacks benefits. It leaves many with an inflexible schedule to find times they can earn income.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="shocks" contentId="caretaking" %}

It's hard to find care for disabled children and middle-aged adults. Long-term care facilities are expensive, and limit options for older family members. Family members providing caretaking support often have to cut back on their work hours and income. This decreases a family's ability to buy healthy food.[1] As a result, households with disabled people are two to three times more likely to be food insecure.[2]

Natural disasters often hit the poor the hardest

California is prone to natural disasters like fires, droughts, and earthquakes. The impact of disaster on low-income communities can be catastrophic and reveal how many people were financially insecure. They may have had enough money to get by, but limited insurance or a very small rainy day fund. Many are forced to use already limited assets to buffer losses like moving to a new area to find a job or home to rent. Following a disaster, aid normally comes in the form of FEMA, unemployment insurance, and food assistance. People who were better off financially often recover more quickly, while others need more support to get back on their feet.

In California, a state with one of the highest costs of living in the country, nearly one in five people live in poverty. CalFresh helps millions of California workers in low-paying jobs buy healthy food during a time of need. CalFresh doesn't only help an individual make it through a difficult time, but also energizes the local economy.
