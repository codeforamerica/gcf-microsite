Why
Californians
need food assistance

The stories behind the numbers

Californians who receive food assistance come from all backgrounds, but many share a similar story: they were barely getting by financially when they were tipped into a crisis by an unexpected expense or loss of income. This site shares their stories.

{% include share.html %}

At Code for America, we run GetCalFresh.org, which helps more than 30,000 households apply for food assistance every month. We've heard again and again how difficult it is for people experiencing hunger to seek out help. Despite their hardships, many of our users describe their reluctance to ask for help. Many tell us that they have never asked for help before and are ashamed about their situation. Applicants feel the stigma that arises from deeply ingrained myths about poverty and welfare in American culture.

Our users' stories challenge these myths. They reflect the common financial struggles that lead people to seek out food assistance. We know that, as one of our users put it, "The less fortunate are invisible to so many people." Our aim is to surface their stories, with dignity and respect, in order to create a more accurate and memorable picture of the people who seek out and receive food assistance in California.

{% include stats.html sectionId="intro" %}

Most GetCalFresh users were barely making ends meet when they were tipped into crisis by an unexpected expense, job loss, or cut in hours.{% include footnote.html number=4 %} {% include footnote.html number=5 %} Once cost of living is taken into account, California is the state with the highest poverty rate. {% include footnote.html number=6 %} Many Californians — working adults, families with children, students, seniors — live precarious financial lives, described in the stories in the first section of this site.

A financial shock, such as an illness or injury, a job loss, a rent increase, or even a car breakdown, might be enough to force a financially vulnerable household to make choices between food and other necessities. The second section of this site includes stories of adverse events that led Californians to seek out food assistance.

The reason people feel shame in asking for help is that they prefer to be self-sufficient. A common goal among users is to be on CalFresh for the shortest possible time. Others mention anxiety that friends, extended family, or employers might find out that they are seeking food assistance.

{% include hero_header.html sectionId="instability" title="Barely getting by" subtitle="Many Californians struggle to make ends meet" %}

Food assistance is a way of coping with a challenge that almost everyone is familiar with: making ends meet. Despite its size and economic diversity, California is the fourth most expensive place to live in the country, after Hawaii, Washington DC, and New York. {% include footnote.html number=30 %}

{% include stats.html sectionId="instability" %}

These economic circumstances mean that many Californians can't quite manage their monthly expenses. The most common challenge GetCalFresh users mention in their applications is not being able to afford food after paying for rent, utilities, and transportation to get to work.

Working, but still hungry

Most of the people receiving CalFresh benefits who can work, do work. But they often work in low-wage jobs like retail, the restaurant industry, home health, or construction. These jobs often have unsteady hours that can leave workers in financially unstable situations. {% include footnote.html number=31 %} Many lack benefits and flexibility, which can mean frequent job losses, fluctuating and insufficient hours, and gaps between jobs if transportation or child care falls through. Food insecurity for these workers harms the health of whole families.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="instability" contentId="parents" %}

Despite their efforts, parents often struggle to provide healthy food for their kids. The median GetCalFresh parent works 38 hours a week, earning $14.70 an hour. In fact, 66% of GetCalFresh households with children include a working adult. Nationally, 65% of SNAP households with children include someone who is working. {% include footnote.html number=32 %}

The testimonials of these parents echo the research findings. In interviews with 90 families with children receiving SNAP benefits, academics found that about half restricted their own eating to make sure their children would have enough food. {% include footnote.html number=33 %} In the U.S. in 2017, 15.7% of households with children were food insecure. {% include footnote.html number=34 %} In about 45% of these households (or 7% of all U.S. households), adults reported skipping meals or cutting the size of a meal in the last 30 days. {% include footnote.html number=35 %}

Just getting started: students and young adults

Many young adults, whether enrolled in college or starting their career, lack the income they need to meet their basic needs.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="instability" contentId="students" %}

Students often take desperate measures to stay fed: they join clubs for the food, participate in research for cash, and ask friends to swipe them into the dining hall. In some counties, a large share of GetCalFresh applicants are students. For example, students account for 60.7% of applications in Santa Cruz County and 58% of applications in Yolo County.

Young people entering the workforce experience the least secure, lowest paid period of their careers. SNAP can serve as a bridge to get them through. GetCalFresh applicants below age 30 work four fewer hours a week (32 vs. 36 hours/week), and earn two dollars less per hour ($13 vs. $15/hour), than applicants in their thirties. Moreover, 46.1% of applicants under age 30 live by themselves, compared to only 28.5% of applicants in their thirties. {% include footnote.html number=36 %}

Seniors facing stigma

Of all Californians, seniors have the greatest gap between their eligibility for SNAP and their enrollment in the program. In California, the average monthly benefit for a senior is $158 {% include footnote.html number=37 %}, making it especially worth their while to apply. Yet they represent the population with the greatest gap between their eligibility for SNAP and their enrollment in the program, due in large part to stigma. {%include footnote.html number=38 %} {%include footnote.html number=39 %} {%include footnote.html number=40 %} {%include footnote.html number=41 %} Nationally, SNAP reaches 85% of all eligible individuals, but only 45% of eligible seniors. {%include footnote.html number=42 %} In California, the disparity is worse: SNAP reaches 70% of all eligible individuals, but only 18% of eligible seniors. {%include footnote.html number=43 %}

With rising costs of living and the severe impacts of the 2007-2009 financial crisis on retirement savings among those approaching retirement age, many seniors do not have enough saved to have a stable retirement income.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="instability" contentId="seniors" %}

As people age, they often struggle with declining physical or mental capacity to work. Many feel compelled to stop working, reduce their hours, or change jobs to cope with a major health crisis of their own or in their household. Even without health challenges, people in late middle age and over frequently report encountering age discrimination in the labor market. Perhaps unsurprisingly, many GetCalFresh users who are seniors mention unemployment as a reason for applying.

{% include hero_header.html sectionId="shocks" title="Common Shocks" subtitle="Common shocks that create a need for CalFresh" %}

Up to this point, we've focused on stories from some of our largest groups of applicants: working families, students or young people starting their careers, and seniors. They convey stress. Their households have no slack in their incomes — they already cannot keep up with the bare necessities. A single car accident, medical emergency, or unexpected layoff can break the budget for months, often forcing them to choose between food, housing, or medical care. In this section, we examine some of the common shocks that lead already vulnerable individuals to seek out CalFresh.

Academic researchers lump shocks that lead to food (and financial) insecurity into simple categories: as sharp increases in expenses or reductions in income.[1][2] In this section, we'll describe a few of the more common shocks our applicants describe. They include losing a job, the high costs of illness or injury, or having to stay home to take care of a family member. Natural disasters, while less frequent, can also be a shock - in recent months, thousands of people have applied due to being displaced by California's wildfires.

{% include stats.html sectionId="shocks" %}

Job loss is the most common reason for applying

For people who are already struggling financially, a job loss or reduction in hours is often the shock that makes food assistance necessary.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="shocks" contentId="job_loss" %}

People who apply for food assistance often have experienced a hardship that causes them to slip into poverty. Roughly 2 in 5 households that fall into poverty do so because of the loss of a job. Nearly half (47%) of households that exit poverty will do so because of a new job. [1]

When transitioning between jobs, CalFresh reduces stress around food availability and the pressure to make costly trade-offs to get by. Clients share stories about dire choices they have to make like selling a car in a rush or using rent money for food and being evicted. The most common reason applicants tell us for no longer needing food assistance is that they went from unemployed to employed, found a better job, or got more hours. CalFresh provides the stability to find more regular hours, or a higher paying job.

Illness and injury are often destabilizing events

Not everyone has a plan when serious illness or disability strikes. Taking time away from work and managing medical bills add to the financial strain of Californians living close to poverty line.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="shocks" contentId="medical" %}

Twenty-eight percent of non-elderly adults on SNAP have a disability.[1] Yet, because most disability benefit applications (SSI or SSDI) are rejected on their first attempt and it often takes two years to get approved, many rely on SNAP in the interim. Research shows that of people who are disabled and on SNAP, two in five do not receive SSI or SSDI.[2]

Choosing between caring for a family member and working

GetCalFresh applicants often describe how much time they spend caring for a disabled spouse, child, or older relative. Caretaking can be a full-time job, but is often unpaid and lacks benefits. It leaves many with an inflexible schedule to find times they can earn income.

{% include quotes.html sectionId="shocks" contentId="caretaking" %}

It's hard to find care for disabled children and middle-aged adults. Long-term care facilities are expensive, and limit options for older family members. Family members providing caretaking support often have to cut back on their work hours and income. This decreases a family's ability to buy healthy food.[1] As a result, households with disabled people are two to three times more likely to be food insecure.[2]

Natural disasters often hit the poor the hardest

California is prone to natural disasters like fires, droughts, and earthquakes. The impact of disaster on low-income communities can be catastrophic and reveal how many people were financially insecure. They may have had enough money to get by, but limited insurance or a very small rainy day fund. Many are forced to use already limited assets to buffer losses like moving to a new area to find a job or home to rent. Following a disaster, aid normally comes in the form of FEMA, unemployment insurance, and food assistance. People who were better off financially often recover more quickly, while others need more support to get back on their feet.

{% include hero_header.html sectionId="calfresh" title="How CalFresh Helps" subtitle="CalFresh helps people get back on their feet" %}

In California, a state with one of the highest costs of living in the country, nearly one in five people live in poverty. CalFresh helps millions of California workers in low-paying jobs buy healthy food during a time of need. CalFresh doesn't only help an individual make it through a difficult time, but also energizes the local economy.
